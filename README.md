# Notes on scaling "no-manager" culture — written while applying to Supabase

I recently applied for the People Business Partner role at Supabase. As part of thinking through what that role might actually involve, I went deep on Supabase's public culture writing — Ant Wilson's ["Who We Hire"](https://supabase.com/blog/who-we-hire) and ["How We Launch"](https://supabase.com/blog/supabase-how-we-launch), plus Paul Copplestone's recent interviews (particularly the [Evil Martians podcast](https://evilmartians.com/events/podcast-paul-copplestone-supabase)).

What started as research turned into two short pieces of writing. I'm sharing them here in case they're useful — either to anyone else thinking about scaling flat, async, "no-manager" cultures, or to anyone at Supabase who wants to read them, agree, or push back.

A quick note on framing: this isn't an attempt to tell Supabase how to operate, or to suggest anything is currently broken. It's an attempt to understand *why* the original operating system worked so well, using Supabase's own words wherever possible — and to think through what the next-scale version of that same system might look like, written by someone who'd genuinely like to help build it.

## What's here

**[`01-strategic-diagnosis.md`](./01-strategic-diagnosis.md)**
*The Supabase Management Paradox* — why the flat, async, founder-driven model worked, what specifically changes as the team doubles, and six things (context, culture, user proximity, process gardening, alignment, writing) that quietly need a new owner as the org outgrows the assumptions the original model was built on.

**[`02-manager-operating-system.md`](./02-manager-operating-system.md)**
*The Supabase Manager Operating System* — the practical companion. For each of the six things above: what a manager can actually do, day to day, to cover the gap — without adding meetings, tools, or process for its own sake. Includes a one-page quick-reference at the end.

## A caveat, in the spirit of the thing itself

Everything here is built from public material — blog posts, podcast transcripts, job postings, status pages. I don't have inside knowledge of how things actually feel day-to-day at Supabase right now, and I'd genuinely expect plenty of this to be already-known, already-in-progress, or just wrong in places. If so, I'd love to hear about it — that's kind of the point.
